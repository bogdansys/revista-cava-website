Limbi Straine

What makes a movie star?

Everybody says, ‘You impress me as a guy who never wanted to be a movie star’. I say: ‘Everybody in the world wants to be a movie star’. -Rip Torn

I must have been around 8 or 9 when I spit it out: “Mom, I’m gonna be a movie star some day!” and years later, I can still hear her heartbreaking laugh. Cause the TV is just such a nice view from the couch, and makes you feel like your world has nothing to offer compared to the big screen. That movie world smells like fantasy, sounds like poetry; so when you have knots woven inside your mind and probably misread feelings, you want to forget your running-on-a-loop reality. Therefore, at some point, you formed a funny idea: that you might have a shot in the seventh art, that you might belong in that distant realm.

The concept of cinema lives as the deepest form of art- closer to life; to human emotion than any other form. A perfect replica of both the pain, and the pleasure of our lives, representing fully the entanglement of animal and god that we are. It’d be foolish to stand against the impact of filmmaking; to somehow deny the ways it has shaped our recent history( from the big, bad boys of Russian propaganda during WW1 to Dick Cheney’s America, to the hardships of third-world colonies, to space exploration, to drug abuse, to censorship and sex, to the notion of tolerance, to the banking system, to the crumbs of modern religions, to the infinity of our potential and to living up to the promises made along the way).

Yet before this great influence, came humble beginnings of motion pictures- where the early 1900’s viewed actors as comedians, clowns, ringmasters at most. I mention this, as it’s important to realize, that there was a time when there was no such thing as a movie star. Of course, those times didn’t last too long. With the inclusions of sound and color on multiple reels, the 30’s were a decade where anonymous movie actors rose from the ashes: from the nickelodeon theatre, to high above the stereotype of being a modest, childlike guilty pleasure of the pre-war working class. Now the 40’s bring life, money, power to the production companies, and the public goes from solely American to global suddenly: the whole world was watching westerns and musicals. So these big production companies, more famously known as the Hollywood studios, start to get greedy. One could make a case that it happened because the studio heads were Jewish, but then we might as well call any money-hungry fellow Jewish! Whatever the case, getting greedy was a problem that needed a solution: and that’s when the old ‘star system’ came in rescue.

The ‘star system’ is an interesting concept, easy to understand, but containing a more subconscious layer than you’d think: this system involves the creation and management of publicity about key performers, or stars, to stimulate demand for their films. While using stars to expand the profile of a movie in the entertainment’s marketplace, producers and casting directors transform stars into some form of investment or capital deployed in anticipation of future profits. The German theorist Walter Benjamin argued that the image of the star compensated the film audience for the loss of direct access to live performance. For this reason, film actors from movie to movie are likely to be cast in similar roles; and from the opposite perspective of the same consequence: the movie becomes the star itself- so there is no “High Society”, there is only a Grace Kelly movie; there is no “East of Eden”, there is only a James Dean movie( let’s pretend we know only one guy with this name!).

If you think that the ‘star system’ sounds like making puppets that talk and dance, then you’re right, but if you thought that it didn’t work, then you’re wrong. Cause it did work- big time. From the 40’s to the 60’s, the film industry’s talent was nothing but a puzzle of glamorised, under surveillance, mostly fake personas of promising theatre youth, crafted carefully with attractive backgrounds by the studios. For example, Cary Grant was born Archibald Leach; Joan Crawford was born Lucille Fay LeSeur.

In a way, the studios’ message was sweet and hopeful: just be young, pretty and lucky- and we’ll do the rest! What made that generation of movie stars was a fame built brick by brick, from a foundation of pure luck, with maybe a bit of talent: ‘I used to think as I looked out on the Hollywood night: there must be thousands of girls sitting alone like me, dreaming of becoming a movie star. But I’m not going to worry about them: I’m dreaming the hardest.’ – Marilyn Monroe. Maybe that’s the secret- a movie star is made by a dream; are you dreaming hard enough would be the question.

The ‘star system’ created movie stars and replaced the shows of Broadway for good: there was no more time for fooling around, there was only time for movies. And just like that, the so called King of Hollywood, Clark Gable, ended the 60’s with a few words: ‘Well, of course I’ve never been consulted; I’m not paid to think!’

From the outside we might see the good and the bad, but thank god we can’t see the ugly. We want, maybe even need, to idolatrize some things, and in a godless civilization, we’ve turned the famous and the rich into gods: ‘If we were a primitive society, movie stars would be gods’- Sydney Pollack.

The conspiratorial aspect of the ‘star system’, as in manipulating images and reality, eventually began to falter- but not because of the people on the outside, but the very ones trapped inside of it. Olivia de Havilland, Bette Davis, Shirley MacLaine sued their studios to be free of their gag orders. Stars all over Hollywood began to show selectiveness with their roles. Contractual disputes were the most popular words in the industry. The oppression, if once at peak, was now gone: ‘What goes up, must come down’-Isaac Newton.

The 60’s brought many changes- many that had nothing to do with the filmmaking industry, but influenced it terribly. So let’s name some events of the 60’s that covered America in dust: the first star of the Hollywood Walk of Fame, JFK, The Vietnam War, Muhammad Ali, marijuana and cocaine, Yuri Gagarin, civil rights, Woodstock, peak of television. So when production companies began changing hands, the studio system could not resist the alterations of culture, labour and entertainment, and as it started shaking during such a strong earthquake, it had the option of evolving into something else: something that works till this day- talent agencies. Better believe that all of today’s big talent agencies started in the 70’s: CAA, WMA(William Morris Agency), ICM(International Creative Management).

In fugitive words, this contemporary stardom plays by the same rules: it feeds on the ease of selling a packaged group of actors to a particular film, ensuring that certain fan groups will offer themselves as a public, no questions asked, therefore reducing risk of failure and increasing profits.

So what makes a movie star? Maybe it’s been us all along- the public. We tell ourselves the lie that we have no power to influence the world, and maybe we might just be the element that moves it. And maybe the metaphor is true: ‘Everybody in the world wants to be a movie star’- everybody wants to be important, to mean something to someone; everybody wants to be somebody. So whatever you want to be in this life, even if it’s a damned movie star…leave with one thought:

‘Don’t tell me I can’t do it! Don’t tell me it can’t be done!’

-‘The Aviator',2004.
