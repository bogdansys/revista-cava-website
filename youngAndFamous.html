Young and Famous

Young and Famous - a blessing or a curse

Life in the spotlight seems so very glamorous – until it isn’t. We’ve all seen the documentaries – monsters hiding in plain sight, contracts that no one benefits from, the intrigues and games in the backstage. Yet, somehow, the life of celebrities has maintained its appeal; no one is thrown off, most want it all the same. And so, looking at the celebrities that grew up in the spotlight, the so-called “child stars”, it seems inexplicable how someone who has everything – money, fame, and influence – can behave the way they do, can be ungrateful and caution against public lives.

The harsh reality is that child stars grow up much sooner than they should. Instead of having normal childhoods and going outside to play, they learn how to answer questions in interviews and what topics to avoid. They must behave appropriately and every mistake they make is sanctioned, because they are role models for other impressionable young children. And while one might say that this is valid for any public figure, normal children rarely worry about how their behavior is perceived by strangers on the street, let alone all over the world.

This explains much of the rebellious behavioral patterns that many young stars exhibit in their late teens and early adulthood; they often test the limits and, occasionally, even end up having trouble with the law. Look no further than Justin Bieber; in his late teens, he was a nuisance to his neighbors, and he even got arrested. And he is not the only example; Macauley Culkin and Lindsey Lohan are only two more household names that had similar issues. But why?

The problem is that having a restricted youth slowly but surely leads to a need for release. Apart from the social responsibility to their public that I mentioned earlier, child stars deal with many other factors that one would typically associate with the adult world, like money, contracts, managers and publicity teams. More often than not, celebrities are seen as money factories, and kids are no exception. They have brand deals, they promote the content they create, they are deprived of their private lives and they are constantly under the pressure to be, simply put, perfect. It is clear that this is a very unhealthy way to live from a mental standpoint, but these are the conditions for the careers they desire. Combined with an abrupt rise to fame and no time to accommodate to the new lifestyle, it is a recipe for disaster.

And so, when they are old enough to answer for their actions, when their parents become less involved, the perfect young celebrities veer off track. The world expresses its disappointment, they are thrown away, and the new sensation takes their place.

On the other side of the specter, there are those who grow up in front of audiences, trying so hard to disprove the idea that child stars are rude, entitled, and ill-behaved that they are taken advantage of. Daniel Radcliffe claimed in an interview for the Graham Norton show that he was so desperate to prove that he is a good person despite being a child sensation that he let people be extremely rude to him. He felt like he had to go against what everyone expected of him, and he admitted that it was a dangerous and unhealthy thing. Moreover, he recounted that his Harry Potter co-star, Rupert Grint, has a very similar problem, but is somehow worse, having ended up going to a fan’s house because he despises refusing people, thinking that he would be rude if he says no.

All in all, it is clear that being a child star is harmful to one’s development on a psychological level. The pressures of this lifestyle weigh down on kids whose only concern at that point in life should be what toy to play with after school, and the ensuing mistakes, often no greater than those of any of their anonymous peers, are condemned, and ruin the careers they sacrificed their normalcy for.
