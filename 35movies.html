35 Movies that break the 4th wall

Let’s take a square room- got 4 standing walls. There’s an actor drinking water inside of it. How can we film the action? If we don’t move the camera, then we can only film the action from one perspective, while covering the wall behind us. So the actor uses three walls, because the camera occupies the fourth. The actor never looks into the camera, so he never uses the fourth wall.

But if he looks into the camera, then we say he’s breaking the fourth wall. Suddenly, the actor becomes aware of us: the audience. He is no longer fictional. He is quite real. We are quiet shocked. He breaks into our realm.

Overall, it’s like a hug, or a pat on the back- the character looks into your eyes and seems to say: “We’re in this together”. It gives the audience an emphasizing lesson. The camera is guiding you with whispers: “Allow yourself to feel this. Live right here, right now.” And the camera isn’t a fool, because when you bought a movie ticket, you swore to offer emotional investment; to offer understanding and to take what you are given. You are given a vision, an idea, a reinterpretation of an idea. Something new instead of the old. Life as you know it, but not quite the same. You are given a film, and that is the most entertaining branch of art that has ever been.

No matter the film’s genre or decade, it deserves a state of undivided attention and trust. Breaking the fourth wall is simply a narrative device that is meant to build a stronger bound with the audience, while maintaining and drawing your attention.

As for the director, well he has to make a choice: to choose this device or not to? Here are some directors that chose it and their products of entertainment.

Portrayals of breaking the fourth wall: in comedies, dramas, romances

1. Most famous example might just be “Ferris Bueller’s Day Off”(1986). It became especially memorable for its end, when the rolling credits brought almost an insult to the watchers: “You’re still here? It’s over. Go home. Go.” DC fans ought to remember 2016’s “Deadpool” mimicking credits.

2. Woody Allen’s “Annie Hall”(1977) is what you call a classic in this business. (“I never want to belong to any club that would want someone like me for a member”; “I used to be a heroin addict. Now I’m a methadone addict”)

3. The film considered nothing less than the “king of comedy”: “Monty Python and the Holy Grail”(1975) (“Oh, I am enjoying this scene”)

4. ”Spaceballs”(1987) (“What the hell am I looking at?”)

5. “Trading Places”(1983) (where Eddie Murphy gets a racially biased lesson about the stock market, and he looks at us with a look that says a lot, without saying anything)

6. ”Kiss, kiss, bang, bang”(2005), in which Robert Downey Jr. wants to leave us with a cute sum up.(“Sorry we said fuc* so much”)

7. “Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas”(1988) (where Johnny Depp gives the audience a run for their lives with some proper cocaine-induced behavior, transforming it into an underrated lesson of “don’t do drugs”, just like “Requiem for a dream”, “Kids”, “City of god”, the A-list casted “Traffic” and “Gia”)

8. ”24 Hour Party People”(2002)

9. The black and white “County Hospital” from 1932.

10. In the criminally underrated “Jay and Silent Bob Strike Back”(2001), the acting and screenwriting Oscar-winning duo of Matt Damon and Ben Affleck meet again.

11. The cliché of “Amelie”(2001), as a French, adorable romantic-comedy (“I like looking back at people’s faces in the dark”)

12. ”Filth”(2013) with a drugged, but still handsome James McAvoy

13. The cliché of ”American Psycho”(2000), as the image of the third millennium’s materialism

14. ”High Fidelity”(2000), also at the turn of the century, but a light, charismatic and realistic romance (“I miss the way she smells, the way she tastes.”)

15. ”Funny Games”(2007) (where the psychopath rewinds a scene with a remote control, thus breaking the fourth wall in a very unique manner)

16. “JFK”(1991)

17. “We’re the Millers”(2013), starring the niece of Julia Roberts and a stripping Jennifer Anniston, is one of the funniest modern comedies.

18. “Wayne’s World”(1992) is simply a fun movie, where we are treated like Wayne’s old-time buddies.

19. Another classic comedy, with the “Dumb and Dumber” type of humor: “Airplane”(1980) (“What a pisser!”)

20. ”I, Tonya”(2017) is based on a true story, starring an angry domestic-abuser Sebastian Stan

21. “A Christmas Story”(1983)

22. “Gremlins 2: The New Batch”(1990)

23. ”How the Grinch Stole Christmas”(2000) (”I guess I could use a little…social interaction”)

24. The cliché of “Fight club”, as a self-discovery journey (“Nobody knows that they saw it, but they did”)

25. ”Alfie”(1966)

26. The infamous “Wolf of Wall Street”, based on a true story that should teach viewers never to invest in penny stocks.(“Was all this legal? Absolutely fuc*ing not. But we were making more money than we knew what to do with it.”)

27. ”Kuffs”(1992)

28. “Pretty in pink”(1986)

29. Whatever the list might be about, “Goodfellas” will always find a place on it. Rarely do we find intellectual filmmaking combined with a fast-paced plot, so to this film we bow. At the end of the movie, when Henry Hill is in court, he uses the fourth wall to connect with the audience when telling his story.

30. “Joy”(2015) always makes me cry from happiness

31. Clockwork Orange(1971)

32. “Full Frontal”(2002). I’ve always loved Soderbergh’s work.

33. “Adaptation”(2002). I’ve always loved Nicolas Cage, even if no one else does.

34. Just a little bit of Tarantino flavour from 2007’s “Death Proof”

35. Sunset Boulevard(1950) with the most famous line in the backstage of Hollywood: “I’m ready for my close-up!”)

Personal favorites

“Lord of War” (2005)
“The Big Short” (2015)
