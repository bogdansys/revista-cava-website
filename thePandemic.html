Literature

The pandemic of being utterly miserable

Sadness. Emptiness. The unbearable depressive mood that consumes us on our worst days. The out-of-the-blue rock bottom, our lowest. We have tried everything: cures, making money and even spending it, so why is it that we feel this inexplicable way?

From the raucous alarm that wakes us up systematically each morning, the monotony of 9 to 5s or school schedules, road rage in heavy city traffic, to hours spent in front of screens of different sizes, the rise in this globally concerning gloom is no surprise. We are living in an extraordinarily technologically advanced time. We have the facilities, opportunities to do so much, but feel as though we can do nothing. Things such as college, previously thought to be inaccessible to most of us, are opening their doors left and right. We are young, healthy, and educated, yet still we feel this way. Our life conditions have gotten better, but our moods have gotten worse. If we seem to be a bunch of ingrates, it may be because the things that used to bring us joy just don’t anymore, whether we are ready to accept that or not.

In such a medically advanced time, when we practically have a cure for almost any disease and we don’t simply die of common colds, we are provided with mental health support and encouraged to take care of ourselves physically as well as mentally. Life expectancy has significantly lengthened and many of our previous worries concerning illnesses have vanished, so why is it that over a third of the population is on antidepressants. The result of googling any symptom we may be experiencing is the whole Internet telling us we are on the verge of death, and so anxiety about our health is spreading among us. So, the insurance of our physical well-being isn’t helping much.

“Money doesn’t buy happiness”, doesn’t it? Some may be more motivated by the exited feeling of owning something brand new, a reward for their hard work or at least something to brag about. For most of us though, the satisfaction brought by purchasing material things is purely ephemeral, a short-time way to lighten our mood. As we constantly feel the need to keep up with trends, influencers, and things such, money will never constitute a way to keep ourselves happy, rather a never-ending loop of buying whatever feels as a necessity, an irreplaceable object new to the market. Thus, money cannot actually cure this disease of dissatisfaction.

Humans are, at their core, extremely social creatures. We constitute one of the many species which feel the need for a sense of membership. Living in these packs, groups, flocks or whatever you want to call them is one of the primal providers of safety and well-being. As much as we enjoy proving ourselves as individual beings, making a point in being original and well-known, the worldwide lack of interpersonal relationships, loved ones, plays a rather big part in the spreading of this miserable mood we suffer from, regardless of our desperate tries. Concluding, the answer is quite an easy one to comprehend: as this feeling sinks in, call a friend.


articol scris de Mihai Anastasia
