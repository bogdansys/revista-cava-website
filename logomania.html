Fashion

The rebirth of logo mania

When you think logos, you either think tacky or statement. From hypebeast culture to subtle wealth show offs, logos have been playing a huge part in the fashion industry for a century. Besides monogram, a myriad of luxury houses use iconic fabric prints, distinguishable from afar, such as Versace’s yellow baroque motifs, Louis Vuitton’s Damier Ebène, you know, the brown squares, and Burberry’s check print.

Now, let’s do a quick history recap:

1896- marks the birth of the Louis Vuitton logo, thus the birth of monogram in fashion. It was actually created in hopes of stopping products from being sold in the counterfeit market, but that’s a story for another time.

The 1980’s-1990’s- Dapper Dan happened. Essentially the father of logo-mania, Dapper Dan made clothing drenched in famous logos for outcasts and rebels. He did eventually have to close down his boutique, as Fendi won a lawsuit against him for using their logo in his creations.

Dapper Dan’s iconic Louis Vuitton jackets



1994- brings Supreme to the market and draws global attention to skateboarding culture.

The late 2000s’- saw a staggering growth in logo-mania, thanks to social media and the pressure put on youngsters to show the world their ‘success’.

The 2010s’-focused on lack of originality and tackiness. We can all agree that the infamous Gucci belt and white logo t-shirt should only be talked about in past tense.

Interestingly, the peak of logo-mania was reached by Virgil Abloh’s new label Off-white, which was devoured by hypebeasts all around the world. Why, you may ask? Limited collaborations, the instantly recognizable crossed arrow logo and famous ‘industrial feel’ (I.e., uncut tags and plastic zip ties) all played part in convincing impressionable teenagers to make irrational purchases that filled their inferiority complex temporarily. Trust me, I was one of them.

Now, what you are all here for.

Let’s talk about the present situation. As all trends come and go, simple logo tees, hoodies and belts became the epitome of tackiness, preponderantly due to the vast number of counterfeit products that turned a 300$ symbol of wealth into a 5$ bazaar dupe.

Fortunately, or unfortunately, depending on who you ask, logo-mania is not dead. After a 5-year break of indulging into anything logo, your average consumer is now once again interested in monogram. The trend shift is visible (from outer space) on the runway, from the reintroduction of the classic Gucci canvas print in its Cruise 2020 show and the birth of monogram-focused fashion houses like Marine Serre, to never seen before collaborations such as Gucci x Balenciaga and Fendi x Versace.

Marine Serre monogram



It is no secret that such unexpected collaborations would get everyone talking, which leads me to my next idea: marketing. As fashion could only reinvent itself so much, designers are running out of ideas to be innovative while still catering to the mass population with plain-structured and wearable pieces.

Being only a means to an end, the two collaborations in discussion went with the same concept: the famous design or silhouette of one’s label with the other’s monogram. The most obvious example might be Balenciaga’s hourglass bag with Gucci’s canvas print, or Fendi’s baguette bag in Versace’s monogram. As if that weren’t enough, Fendi and Versace took it up a notch, creating the ‘ship’ name ‘Fendace’, the simplest solution to setting a historical moment in fashion.

At first thought, the elegance and neutral tones of Fendi might make you think would pair hazardously with the metallics, safety pins and baroque motifs of Versace. While the show had its definite misses, clearly designed for the whole purpose of mass-consumption, merging chain mail fabric with embellished Fendi logos as well as tailored suits with satin prints were not such a bad idea. Afterall, this collaboration was the middle ground for maximalism and grace.



On the other side of the spectrum, Gucci’s boldness and grandiosity were at its heights in the Balenciaga collab. Everything was drenched in logos, to the point where tackiness became a fashion statement.



Logos don’t always have to be in our face. Take for example Maison Margiela’s logo history, which started out as a white square, sewn with four loose stitches in order to be able to easily remove the tag and keep the sense of mystery and anonymity. The current tag is still focusing on anonymity, as only fashion enthusiasts can recognise a Margiela tag. Moreover, the numbers 0-23 represent Margiela’s collections, with one of the numbers circled depending on which line the piece came from.

Prada is another great example of subtle, yet noticeable logos. With Raf Simons accompanying Miuccia Prada as co-creative director, structured, solid-coloured pieces have taken over the runway, with subtle references to Prada's iconic triangle in the garments’ neckline, gloves’ pocket etc.

All in all, logos can be done in two ways depending on their purpose. Should an individual wish to show his shallow ‘wealth’ to another group of individuals whom react with envy, Supreme, Off White and general logo-mania is the way to go. But if the individual wishes to purchase a good quality garment, whilst still justifying its price to his peers, subtle hints of ‘poshness’ are the answer.




articol scris de Alessia Grecu
