GRAMMYs or SCAMMYs?

The 63rd edition of the GRAMMY Awards has had the music industry in a continuous uproar since the nomination announcements and, with the winners finally in possession of their prizes, it seems as though the distaste of the public has been given renewed strength. In all honesty, the GRAMMY Awards have long been under the scrutiny of artists and fans alike, and referring to them as SCAMMYs due to their lack of transparency and diversity is a common occurrence on all social media platforms. So why is the Recording Academy receiving so much criticism now for the same practices they have implemented for decades?

The catalyst for this continuous backlash against the GRAMMYs seems to have been the already famous snub that the Weekend suffered at this edition. After what was unarguably the best year of his career, everyone was shocked to learn that he did not receive a single nomination from the Academy, and he made his feelings clear on the matter by deciding to no longer submit his music to the GRAMMYs lest the secret committees should be disbanded. Be that as it may, it is hardly the first time that deserving artists did not receive the recognition they deserved from the committees. Big names such as Taylor Swift, Rihanna, and ZAYN have notoriously not been given a nod of acknowledgment in past ceremonies, with the latter speaking out in support of the Weekend just a few weeks ago.

The harsh truth is that, like most things and despite superhuman efforts to keep the awards fair, the GRAMMYs have been anything but that lately. While it is clear that an artist with a successful year may take home more than one trophy, that is a far cry from the reality of the same people winning all of the big categories and “sweeping” the ceremony. As though finally aware of the issues of the public with their predictability, the Academy made it a point to share the prizes between as many people as possible this year. It did not work.

If the nominations were bad, the awards themselves were a disaster.

Megan Thee Stallion was the shock of the night, going home with no less than three GRAMMYs (Best New Artist, Best Rap Song, Best Rap Interpretation), 2 of them for her song Savage. Regardless of her backstory and the track’s popularity among teenagers on TikTok, a song about opulence and money should have never won a category in which a recording like The Bigger Picture by Lil Baby, talking about real social issues and sending a true message, which is the essence of the rap genre, was nominated. Meanwhile Billie Eilish and Finneas, two of the most critically acclaimed songwriters of this generation who also happen to be at the peak of popularity, only took home two awards. Beyoncé won 4 categories, bringing her to an astounding total of 28 GRAMMYs now, and is the most awarded female artist in history at this ceremony, surpassing the previous record-holder by just one award. Taylor Swift took home the Album of the Year award, making her the first woman to win this distinction three times with three different genres. And while I believe the two are most deserving of these historic moments, it is hard to believe that such household names would have gone home with such a big chance missed regardless of the true value of their work.

It feels as though, in their attempt to even out the playing field and calm the fury of the people, the secret committees forgot to truly cherish art in certain categories and instead declared winners like they were at Nickelodeon’s Teen Choice Awards. What message does this send to the public? The only deserving wins were those of the already established artists, with very few exceptions (for instance, H.E.R). The rest were awarded based on who has the most fans or followers, so as to appease the greatest number of people. Is this the way the Recording Academy feels about judging and valuing music?

The saddest part is that, were it not for the Weekend’s announced boycott in November, nothing would have changed. While the attempt the GRAMMYs made this year ought to be recognized as their effort to change if only for appearances’ sake, it is not enough. As both ZAYN and the Weekend have pointed out, so long as the secret committees are in place, there will never be transparency, and that is what these once distinguished awards are in dire need of to maintain their relevance and importance.
