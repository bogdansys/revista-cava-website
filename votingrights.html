Voting rights - The only aspect a pandemic can't ruin

2020 has been an exhausting year defined mostly by sorrowful events. Being surrounded by tragic situations, as human beings, we tend to overlook the things that made it a bit better. It has been a year marked by elections, not only here in Romania, where we voted for The European Parliament, local councils, and the most recent one, for the Romanian Parliament, but also in the United States, where the population chose their next president in an historic election.

Nowadays, voting is a fundamental right that we take for granted, because we, as teenagers and young adults mostly, have no idea how a life with no right to express our political views does look like. The introduction of universal suffrage happened in France in 1792, a right addressed only to men. The first state in the world to allow all men and women to exert their right to vote was New Zeeland in 1893. Throughout history, voting had been a controversial subject in every country. In Romania’s case, after we united as a whole in 1918, voting applied only to manhood, women being allowed to participate in this political process only after twenty years, in 1948.

Overseas, The United States went through years and years of racial discrimination and male supremacy. Under president Lyndon B. Johnson, in 1965, it was signed the Voting Rights Act. Preceded by several protests and riots, this year represented a new beginning for the country, the barriers which banned people from different races and ethnicities from deciding their country’s political future being removed permanently.

"Men, their rights, and nothing more; women, their rights, and nothing less." — Susan B. Anthony, One of the Most Determined Suffragists

The year 2020 marked the Centenary of Women’s Suffrage in the U.S. Being born in Romania, we can’t completely comprehend the importance of this event, but as a girl, I find this an occasion to show appreciation to those who fought for their rights back in the day and also to realize how privileged we are to be born in a world which benefits us with almost everything we need. Their movement set out an example of determination; therefore, I think that it’s not necessary to be an American citizen to praise this moment in history. The brains of the process of restoring women’s rights in 1848 were Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Matt, who brought over 300 people (including men) to establish the “Declaration of Sentiments”, that enumerated the inequities females had to face in those times: lack of custody of children in case of divorce, no independent rights after marriage, no voice in the law and obviously, no right to vote.

Women were the embodiment of a puppet manipulated by men, who created the ideology that a women’s highest achievement was to keep the household clean and to give birth. Having no access to a higher level of education because the law didn’t allow them to, they were inferior to their partners, who had to be treated with utmost appreciation. Therefore, women needed to speak their minds. But how can you achieve anything when you are playing against people who demolish every basic human right you deserve? It took almost a century and several generations of strong-willed women to finally attain some of their objectives. On August 26th,1920, the triumphant amendment that allowed women to have an impact on their country’s politics by voting had been officially signed for all American states. Righteously, the Women’s Suffrage Movement had been considered to be one of the most noteworthy events in the women’s history alongside the National Society for Women’s Suffrage in the UK – which promoted the same values and celebrated its centenary in 2018. Not only ought we understand their significance over time but learn from the lesson they teach on how matters should be handled in order to produce a social change in a system.

History proves us how hard people fought for their rights in the past. In the 21st century, we can proudly say that our opinion is taken into consideration. We are able to vote, we can go out in the streets and protest, we have the power to make a real difference, but we don’t. As the years go by, the voter turnout decreases notably, but the general view on the current politicians is not pleasing. A democracy bases itself on the election’s outcome; therefore, the lower the voter turnout, the fewer the chances that the election result satisfies the population of potential voters. The reasons people put up for not voting are usually simple: the thoughts that their vote won’t make a change or that their implication won’t be considered. Not having participated in the voting process shows that either people don’t accept the way things work, choosing to complain about it rather than doing something or that they are careless. It is concerning me that these approaches are becoming more and more common these days. Before the elections, when we are given the opportunity to critique our leaders, we don’t hesitate to, but when it’s time for us to turn things around, just by simply giving our vote, we settle for inaction, even though it has been proved in many occasions that inaction doesn’t lead to anything beneficial and is as bad as the wrong action.

Humans have always avoided responsibility and have had the tendency to put the blame on others, being scared to take matters into their own hands. Let’s have a brief moment to discern that our country’s fate depends on us and on our decisions. We are praising those who dedicated their lives to obtaining basic human rights by expressing our opinions frankly, by understanding the role we play in the system, and last but not least, by giving our vote when it’s asked. We chose our governors whom we criticise uninterruptedly and if we can’t take a five-minute walk on the election day to vote them out, then we deserve our faulty leaders.
