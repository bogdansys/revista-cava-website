About The Woman Who Defied Feminism

“Equality of rights under the law shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of sex.” - the Equal Rights Amendment

As second-wave feminism sparks across the United States of America during the 1960s and the Equal Rights Amendment garners increasing support, one would expect women of all social classes to stand up together for their rights. It is thus with much astonishment that we learn about a right-wing woman fighting so fiercely against feminist movements and women’s emancipation, all while claiming that she supports women’s “real rights”.

Conservative activist Phyllis Schlafly has been, by no doubt, a very controversial figure, whose influence and anti-feminist crusade have halted an important amendment to the U.S. Constitution. Born as Phyllis Steward on August 15, 1924 in a Republican family, Phyllis embraced many of her family’s views. Valedictorian of her high-school class, she attended Washington University after transferring from Maryville College, and after graduation (1944), she obtained a master’s degree in government at Radcliff College. In 1949, Phyllis would marry an affluent lawyer named John Schlafly, to whom she would later repeatedly thank for permitting her to get involved in politics. Until the intensification of discussions concerning the ERA, Schlafly had rather been preoccupied with the threat of communism and with the further pursuit of research on nuclear weapons. However, when asked to state her opinion on the matter, she declared herself a strong opponent and considered the amendment a direct attack on the “wife and homemaker”. As a result, she has founded in 1972 the STOP ERA group, which was an acronym for “Stop taking our privileges, Equal Rights Amendment” and whose main goal was to stop the ratification of the Equal Rights Amendment by reminding the American people how privileged the American housewives were. Schlafly has managed to mobilize Christian housewives - who had never been engaged in politics before - and has strongly advocated against her own interpretation of what the ERA represented. She warned women that the amendment would devalue their marriages, would force them to join the military, and that it would take a toll on the traditional family; she also staunchly chastised the rights the ERA would offer to gay people and the legalization of abortion. During her time of fervent advocacy, she urged the opponents of the ERA to present themselves as rather feminine than feminist and kept on pointing out a false narrative of what the ratification of the ERA would signify.

The Equal Rights Amendment, initially proposed by suffragist Alice Paul in 1923 and later rephrased in 1943, would have been the 27th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. Strongly supported and advocated by the National Organization for Women (whose founder was Betty Friedman, author of “The Feminine Mystique”), it had already passed through the Congress and the Senate by 1972, and further needed to be ratified by 38 states within seven years in order to become law. Within the first year, the ERA has been ratified by 30 states, but as Schlafly’s anti-feminist, pro-family campaign has intensified, the ratification was brought to a standstill. In the years that followed, only five more states have ratified the amendment, and even if the deadline for ratification has been extended from 1979 to 1982, Schlafly has triumphantly celebrated her victory by stating that the ERA was "dead for now and forever in this century."

Although she promoted the role of women as mothers and wives and has portrayed herself as a traditional housewife with six children ("When I fill out applications, I put down 'Mother' as my occupation"), there was a real discrepancy between her personal life and her public statements. Running multiple times for Congress and administrating the STOP ERA group – which later became the Eagle Forum – did not mean embracing the housewife stance anymore, and she has been often viewed as a woman who has reached her full potential, and not as the image of the housewife she stood up for. For instance, even feminist Karen DeCrow and former NOW chief once said: “If I had a daughter, I’d like her to be a housewife just like Phyllis Schlafly.”.

Subsequent to her fight against the ratification of the amendment, Phyllis has continued pursuing conservative activism and has militated against feminist movements. In the last few years of her life, she appeared as a strong supporter of another towering controversial figure, Donald Trump, whom she depicted as a family guy. She died on September 5th, 2016 at age 92.

While she is not to be admired, one cannot deny the political prowess she has proven or her ability to mobilize large number of people, to persuade politicians, and to organize grassroots. However, she will forever remain the woman who has fought against feminist movements and who has contributed to the downfall of the Equal Rights Amendment.


articol scris de Ioana Barbu
